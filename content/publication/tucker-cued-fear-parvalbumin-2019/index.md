abstract: "Repeated head trauma is strongly linked to cognitive and emotional disorders, and repetitive concussive brain injuries (rCBI) may be a risk factor for depression and anxiety disorders. In this study, male and cycling female C57BL/6J mice sustained three concussive injuries at 24-hour intervals and were tested in context and cued fear conditioning, open field, elevated zero maze, and tail suspension paradigms. All injured mice showed reduced freezing during contextual fear testing, while injured male mice specifically showed less freezing to the auditory cue. Injured mice were hyperactive in the open field and spent more time in the open quadrants of the elevated zero maze, suggesting decreased anxiety-like behavior, but did not differ from sham controls in depressive-like behavior. Pathologically, injured mice showed increased astrogliosis in cortex and white matter, and injured male mice showed fewer parvalbumin-positive neurons in the hippocampus. These findings suggest sex-specific hippocampal inhibitory changes may contribute to altered fear-related behavior after repetitive concussive injury."
