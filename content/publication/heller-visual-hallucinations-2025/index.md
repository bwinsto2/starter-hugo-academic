abstract: "Visual hallucinations (VH) are a core symptom of both Lewy body diseases (LBDs; for example, Parkinson's disease and dementia with Lewy bodies) and serotonergic psychedelics (SPs; for example, psilocybin and mescaline). While these conditions differ in etiology, overlapping phenomenology and neural mechanisms suggest shared pathways. This review compares VH in LBDs and SPs, focusing on phenomenology, cortical function, and serotonergic modulation. Both LBDs and SPs exhibit visual aberrations ranging from minor metamorphopsias to complex hallucinations, and some LBD features resemble those induced by SPs, suggesting shared mechanisms. Neuroimaging studies indicate a common pattern of hyperactive associative cortex and hypoactive sensory cortex. At the neuromodulator level, SP-induced VH involves 5-HT2A and 5-HT1A receptor modulation, whereas in LBDs, 5-HT2A receptor upregulation correlates with increased VH and its inhibition can reduce hallucinations. Examining these shared cortical and neuromodulatory features may clarify how sensory degradation, excitation, and hallucinogenesis relate across conditions."
